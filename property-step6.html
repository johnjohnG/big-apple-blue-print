Step 6: Rental Maintenance and Repairs in New York

In New York, the law establishes a balance of responsibilities between landlords and tenants. This relationship is governed by state real property laws and local building codes, which ensure that housing remains safe, sanitary, and functional.

The Warranty of Habitability

Every residential lease in New York includes an implied "Warranty of Habitability." This is a non-waivable legal promise that the landlord will keep the premises safe and livable.

  • Livable Standards: This covers essential services such as heat, water, electricity, and protection from the elements.
  • Emergency Conditions: If a condition exists that is dangerous to life, health, or safety (such as a gas leak, a collapsed ceiling, or a complete loss of heat), the landlord is legally obligated to address it immediately.

Landlord Responsibilities

Landlords are responsible for the structural integrity and essential systems of the building:

  • Heat Requirements: From October 1 through May 31, landlords must provide heat when outdoor temperatures fall below specific thresholds. During the day (6 AM – 10 PM), if it is below 55°F outside, the inside temperature must be at least 68°F. At night, the inside must be at least 62°F regardless of outdoor temperatures.
  • Hot Water: Landlords must provide hot water 365 days a year, at a minimum temperature of 120°F.
  • Safety Gear: This includes the installation and maintenance of smoke detectors, carbon monoxide alarms, and window guards (if a child aged 10 or younger lives in the unit).
  • Common Areas: Landlords must maintain clean and safe lobbies, hallways, stairwells, and elevators.

Tenant Responsibilities

Tenants have a legal duty to maintain their unit in a way that does not jeopardize the building or other residents:

  • General Upkeep: Tenants must keep their unit in a clean and sanitary condition to prevent fire hazards or pest infestations.
  • Preventing Damage: While "ordinary wear and tear" is expected, tenants are responsible for repairing damage caused by their own negligence or that of their guests.
  • Providing Access: Tenants must allow landlords or building staff to enter for necessary repairs or inspections, provided the landlord gives "reasonable notice" (generally 24 hours). In cases of emergency, such as a burst pipe, notice is not required.

If the Landlord Fails to Make Repairs

If a landlord neglects their duty to provide a habitable home, tenants have several established legal paths:

  1. Documentation: Always request repairs in writing (email, certified mail, or a tracked management portal). A clear paper trail is essential.
  2. 311 Reports (NYC): Filing a report via 311 creates an official government record. This may trigger an inspection by the Department of Housing Preservation and Development (HPD). If a violation is confirmed, the city can fine the landlord or perform emergency repairs.
  3. HP Action: Tenants can file an "HP Action" in Housing Court to ask a judge to order the landlord to perform specific repairs. The court can set strict deadlines for completion.
  4. Rent Withholding (Use Caution): While the law recognizes the right to withhold rent for serious habitability issues, this is a high-risk strategy. Withholding rent often triggers an eviction case. It is highly recommended to consult a tenant advocacy group or legal professional before doing so to ensure proper procedures are followed.

If the Tenant Fails to Comply

If a tenant causes significant damage, refuses to provide access for essential repairs, or creates a public nuisance, the landlord has legal remedies:

  1. Notice to Cure: The landlord may issue a formal notice identifying the violation and providing a specific timeframe (usually 10 days) for the tenant to fix the problem.
  2. Lease Termination: If the tenant does not resolve the issue after a Notice to Cure, the landlord may issue a "Notice of Termination," which officially ends the lease.
  3. Holdover Proceeding: If a tenant remains after the lease is terminated, the landlord must file a "Holdover Proceeding" in Housing Court to regain possession of the property.
  4. Security Deposit Deductions: Upon move-out, a landlord may legally deduct from the security deposit the "reasonable cost" of repairing damage beyond ordinary wear and tear, provided they provide an itemized list of the costs.
