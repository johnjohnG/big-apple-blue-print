Employment

1. The "At-Will" Rule

In New York, employment is generally “at-will.”

  • What it means: Either you or your employer can end the job at any time, for almost any reason, and without giving advance notice.
  • The Exception: They cannot fire you for a discriminatory reason (like your race, religion, or national origin) or as retaliation for speaking up about your rights.

2. Your Pay: Minimum Wage & Overtime (2026 Rates)

Region Minimum Wage (per hour)
NYC, Long Island, & Westchester $17.00
The rest of New York State $16.00

As of 2026, New York has some of the highest wage protections in the country. These rights apply to all workers, regardless of their immigration status.

Overtime: If you work more than 40 hours in a week, you must be paid "time and a half" (1.5x your hourly rate) for those extra hours.

Tipped Workers: If you work in a restaurant or hotel, your employer can pay you a lower "cash wage" as long as your tips make up the difference to reach the full minimum wage.

3. Essential Paperwork

When you start a job, you will be handed a stack of forms. Here are the big three:

  • Form I-9: This verifies you are legally allowed to work in the US. You will need to show documents (like a Green Card or Work Authorization Card). Note: Employers cannot force you to show a specific document; you choose which ones to show from the official list.
  • Form W-4: This tells the government how much tax to take out of your paycheck.
  • Wage Notice: In NY, your employer must give you a written notice when you are hired that states your pay rate and payday.

4. New Protections for 2026

New York recently added even stronger protections for workers:

  • Credit Check Ban: Starting April 2026, most employers in New York State are prohibited from checking your credit history to decide whether to hire you. This is a huge win for immigrants who are still building their US credit.
  • Paid Safe & Sick Leave: In NYC, you are entitled to time off to care for yourself or a family member, and even for "safe time" if you are dealing with a domestic violence situation.
  • Anti-Discrimination: New laws clarify that even if an employer didn't intend to discriminate, if their policy has a "predictable unfair effect" on a specific group, it is illegal.

5. Red Flags to Watch Out For

🚩
  • "Under the Table" Pay: If an employer offers to pay you only in cash and "off the books," they are likely avoiding taxes and denying you your legal rights to injury insurance (Workers' Comp).
  • Paycheck Deductions: An employer cannot take money out of your pay for things like broken plates, cash register shortages, or "bad work" without your written permission.
  • Promissory Notes: It is now illegal in NY for an employer to make you sign a note promising to "repay" them for your job as a condition of being hired.

6. Helpful Resource:

📞 Need help? If you think your rights are being violated, you can call the New York State Department of Labor at 1-888-4-NYSDOL. They assist workers regardless of immigration status and often provide services in multiple languages.
